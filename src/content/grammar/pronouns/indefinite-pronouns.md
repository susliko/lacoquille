## What it is

Indefinite pronouns refer to unspecified or generic people and things. They don't point to anyone specific. Some are used as subjects, some as objects; some are always positive, others always negative.

## Key forms

### Subject-type indefinites

| Pronoun | Meaning | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| on | we/they/one/you | Most common indefinite; conjugates as 3rd sing |
| chacun | each one | masc, but can refer to mixed groups |
| chacune | each one | feminine form |
| tout le monde | everyone | literally "all the world" |
| quelqu'un | someone | invariable |
| quelques-uns/-unes | some | has masculine/feminine forms |

### Object-type indefinites

| Pronoun | Meaning | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| quelque chose | something | + adjective with de: *quelque chose de beau* |
| rien | nothing | requires ne in negation |
| personne | no one | requires ne in negation |
| aucun/aucune | none, not any | requires ne in negation; agrees in gender |

### Quantity-type

| Pronoun | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| tout, tous | all |
| plusieurs | several |
| certains, certaines | some |
| d'autres | others |

## Usage rules

### On — the everyday "we"

In spoken French, **on** replaces **nous** as the standard "we." It always takes third-person singular verb agreement:

*On est contents.* — We are happy. (literally: "One is happy")

**On** can also mean:
- They (unspecified): *On frappe à la porte.* — Someone is knocking at the door.
- You (generic): *On n'est jamais content.* — You can never please everyone. (One is never content.)
- People in general: *En France, on mange du pain.* — In France, people eat bread.

### Quelqu'un / Quelque chose

These are the "positive" indefinites meaning "someone" and "something." When followed by an adjective, use **de + adjective**:

*Quelque chose de intéressant* — Something interesting
*Quelqu'un de gentil* — Someone kind

### Negation with rien, personne, aucun

These three are **always negative** when used alone. When they appear in a sentence with a verb, the verb must use **ne** (not *pas*) for negation:

*Je ne vois **rien**.* — I see nothing. (not *pas rien*)
*Personne ne **rompt** ce silence.* — No one breaks this silence.
*Aucune **voiture** ne **passe**.* — No car passes.

### Tout le monde

This phrase means "everyone" and is third-person singular:

*Tout le monde **est** là.* — Everyone is here. (not *sont*)

### Tout / Tous

| Form | Use | Example |
|------|-----|---------|
| tout | everything, anything | *Tout **est** possible.* — Everything is possible. |
| tous | all (of them) | *Ils **sont** tous là.* — They are all here. |

Note: *tous* at the end of a sentence is pronounced "tooss" (the s is pronounced), unlike *tout* meaning "everything."

## Common pitfalls

### On vs People/They — translation confusion

English speakers often struggle because **on** doesn't map neatly to English. In French, **on** is the default subject pronoun for "we" in conversation. Don't try to translate it literally — understand it as the natural French way to say "we."

### Double negatives

With negative pronouns, the verb has **ne** but no **pas**:

*Je ne fais rien.* ✓ — I do nothing.
*Je ne fais pas rien.* ✗

This is the opposite of English ("I don't do nothing" = I do something in English, but NEVER in French).

### Tout vs Tous — pronunciation and meaning

*Tout **est** prêt.* — Everything is ready. (pronounce the final t)
*Ils sont **tous** là.* — They are all there. (pronounce the final s as "tooss")

Same spelling, different pronunciation and meaning based on context.
