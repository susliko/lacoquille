## What it is

Reflexive pronouns are used with **reflexive verbs** — verbs where the action returns to the subject. The subject performs an action on itself. The pronoun form matches the subject: me/te/se/nous/vous.

## Key forms

| Subject | Reflexive Pronoun |
|---------|-------------------|
| je | me (m') |
| tu | te (t') |
| il/elle/on | se (s') |
| nous | nous |
| vous | vous |
| ils/elles | se (s') |

## Usage rules

### Agreement in compound tenses

With *être* as the auxiliary in compound tenses (like passé composé), the past participle agrees with the direct object — which in reflexive verbs is the reflexive pronoun when it precedes the verb.

*Elle s'est lavée.* — She washed herself. (se = direct object, feminine → agreement)
*Ils se sont lavés.* — They washed themselves. (se = direct object, masculine plural → agreement)
*Nous nous sommes lavés.* — We washed ourselves.

But if the reflexive pronoun is **indirect** (the action is done *to* or *for* oneself, not *by* oneself), there's no agreement:

*Elle s'est lavé les mains.* — She washed her hands. (les mains = direct object, se = indirect)
(no agreement with se)

### Reciprocal vs Reflexive meaning

**Se** can mean:
- True reflexive: *Je me lave.* — I wash myself.
- Reciprocal: *Nous nous aimons.* — We love each other.

Context makes the meaning clear. Both look identical in form.

### Se as passive (la voix passive)

When se appears with an inanimate subject, the verb takes on passive meaning:

*Ce livre se vend bien.* — This book sells well.
*Les portes se ferment à minuit.* — The doors close at midnight.

This is not reflexive (the subject isn't doing something to itself) — it's a passive construction using se.

### Impersonal se

With certain verbs, **se** appears but has no real subject relationship:

*Il se peut que…* — It may be that…
*Il s'agit de…* — It's about…

These are set expressions — don't try to analyze the reflexive relationship.

## Common pitfalls

### Over-agreeing with reflexive verbs

The past participle agrees with the reflexive pronoun only when the reflexive pronoun is the **direct object**. Many reflexive verbs are actually indirect (the reflexive pronoun is an indirect object), so no agreement occurs:

*Elle s'est douché les cheveux.* ✗ (wrong analysis)
*Elle s'est douché les cheveux.* ✓ (correct — "les cheveux" is the direct object, se is indirect)

Actually, the correct form is: *Elle s'est lavé les cheveux.* — She washed her hair. (les cheveux = direct object, no agreement with se)

Wait, let me clarify: with *laver*, the reflexive construction *se laver quelque chose* means the reflexive pronoun is indirect — you're washing something belonging to yourself. In this case:
- *Elle s'est lavé les cheveux.* — She washed her hair. (les cheveux = direct object, no agreement)
- *Elle s'est lavée.* — She washed (herself). (se = direct object, agrees)

### Choosing between se and non-reflexive

Some verbs exist in both reflexive and non-reflexive forms with different meanings:

*Je souviens ce fait.* ✗
*Je me souviens de ce fait.* ✓ — I remember this fact.

*Elle a aperçu le problème.* — She noticed the problem.
*Elle s'est aperçue du problème.* — She realized the problem.

These must be learned as set combinations.
