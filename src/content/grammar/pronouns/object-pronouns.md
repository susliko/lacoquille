## What it is

Object pronouns replace nouns that receive the action of the verb. French distinguishes between **direct objects** (the thing directly affected) and **indirect objects** (the recipient of the action). The choice of pronoun depends on which role the noun plays.

## Key forms

### Direct object pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | me (m') | nous |
| 2nd | te (t') | vous |
| 3rd | le (l') / la (l') | les |

*Le* is masculine, *la* is feminine, *les* is plural for both.

### Indirect object pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | me (m') | nous |
| 2nd | te (t') | vous |
| 3rd | lui | leur |

Note: **lui** (to him/her) and **leur** (to them) are always indirect objects.

## Usage rules

### Direct vs Indirect distinction

A verb takes a direct object if you can say "I [verb] it" without changing meaning. If you need "I [verb] **to** it," it's indirect.

*Je vois la maison.* → Je **la** vois. (direct — I see it)
*Je parle à Marie.* → Je **lui** parle. (indirect — I speak to her)

### Word order with compound tenses

In compound tenses (like passé composé), the pronoun goes **before the auxiliary**:

*J'ai vu la maison.* → Je **l'ai** vue. ✓
*J'ai parlé à Marie.* → Je **lui ai** parlé. ✓

Wrong: *J'ai vu la maison.* → ~~Je ai l'ai vue.~~ ✗

### Special case with avoir

When a direct object pronoun (le/la/les) appears with avoir in compound tenses, the past participle agrees with the pronoun's gender and number:

*J'ai vu les chats.* → Je **les ai** vus. ✓
*J'ai vu la maison.* → Je **l'ai** vue. ✓

This agreement does NOT happen with indirect object pronouns (lui, leur):

*J'ai parlé aux enfants.* → Je **leur ai** parlé. (no agreement)

## Common pitfalls

### Confusing direct and indirect objects with donner

*Je donne le livre à Marie.* — I give the book to Marie.
- The book = direct object → **le**
- Marie (recipient) = indirect object → **lui**

Correct: *Je **le lui** donne.* ✓
Wrong: *Je **lui le** donne.* ✗ (sounds unnatural)

### Me, te, nous, vous as both

These pronouns can be either direct or indirect depending on the verb:

*Il me voit.* — He sees me. (direct)
*Il me parle.* — He speaks to me. (indirect — speaks to me)

### Agreement with le/la/les + avoir

When the direct object pronoun precedes the verb with *avoir*, the past participle agrees:

*Tu as lu le livre ?* — Tu **l'as** lu ? or Tu **l'as** lue ? (depends on book's gender — livre = masculine, so no -e)

Actually, *livre* is masculine, so: *Tu **l'as** lu.* ✓
*Tu as lue la lettre ?* → Tu **l'as** lue. ✓
