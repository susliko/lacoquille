## What it is

Possessive pronouns replace the combination of possessive adjective + noun. They stand in for the noun to show ownership. Because they replace nouns, they must match the gender and number of the thing being possessed, not the owner.

## Key forms

### Full paradigm (mine / yours / his / hers / ours / theirs)

| Owner | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Masculine Plural | Feminine Plural |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| my | le mien | la mienne | les miens | les miennes |
| your | le tien | la tienne | les tiens | les tiennes |
| his/her/its | le sien | la sienne | les siens | les siennes |
| our | le nôtre | la nôtre | les nôtres | les nôtres |
| your (pl/formal) | le vôtre | la vôtre | les vôtres | les vôtres |
| their | le leur | la leur | les leurs | les leurs |

## Usage rules

### When to use possessive pronouns vs adjectives

**Possessive adjectives** (mon, ma, mes, ton, ta, tes…) modify a noun directly:

*C'est **mon** livre.* — It's my book.

**Possessive pronouns** replace the noun entirely:

*C'est **le mien**.* — It's mine.

Often both appear in the same sentence to make a comparison or contrast:

*Ma voiture est rouge, **la tienne** est bleue.* — My car is red, yours is blue.

### Agreement: the noun possessed, not the owner

The form of the pronoun matches the gender and number of **the thing possessed**, not the owner:

*Ta sœur et ses enfants (masc pl)* → **les miens** (my children, masc pl)
*Ta sœur et ses enfants (fem pl)* → **les miennes** (my sisters, fem pl)

If you're referring to a feminine noun (like "sœur"), you use feminine forms.

### Using le/la/les before the pronoun

Possessive pronouns always start with an article:

*Le mien, la tienne, les siens* — never *mien, tienne, siens*

### After être — no article needed sometimes

When the pronoun alone is the subject with *être*, you may see the article dropped in informal speech:

*C'est **mien**.* (colloquial) vs *C'est **le mien**.* (standard)

Standard French keeps the article.

## Common pitfalls

### Confusing possessive pronouns with adjectives

This confusion is natural since forms look similar:

| Adjective (modifies noun) | Pronoun (replaces noun) |
|---------------------------|-------------------------|
| **mon** livre | **le mien** |
| **ta** sœur | **la tienne** |
| **ses** enfants | **les siens** |

If a noun follows, it's the adjective. If no noun follows, it's the pronoun.

### Their vs Theirs in French

French doesn't have "their" as a possessive pronoun standing alone. You must use **le leur / la leur / les leurs**:

*Ce sont les enfants. C'est **le leur**.* — These are the children. They are theirs.

### Third person possessives (son/sa/ses vs le sien/la sienne)

**Son/sa/ses** = possessive **adjective** (agrees with thing possessed):
*Son livre* (his book or her book — ambiguous in French)

**Le sien/la sienne/les siens** = possessive **pronoun** (replaces noun + adjective):
*Le livre est **le sien**.* — The book is his/hers.

The ambiguity in *son livre* is resolved by context or by switching to the pronoun form.
