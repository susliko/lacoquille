## What it is

The definite articles **le**, **la**, and **les** mean "the." They tell the listener you're referring to something specific — something both of you can identify.

## Forms

| Gender | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Masculine | le | les |
| Feminine | la | les |

- *le livre* — the book (masculine)
- *la table* — the table (feminine)
- *les enfants* — the children (plural)

## Contractions with à and de

Two important contractions happen with **à** (to/at) and **de** (of/from):

| Contraction | Full form | Example |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| au | à + le | *au marché* — to the market |
| aux | à + les | *aux enfants* — to the children |
| du | de + le | *du travail* — of/from the work |
| des | de + les | *des amis* — of/from the friends |

- *Je parle **aux** étudiants.* — I'm speaking to the students.
- *Je viens **du** supermarché.* — I come from the supermarket.

## L' before vowels

**le** and **la** become **l'** before a vowel or mute h:

- *l'eau* (feminine) — the water
- *l'homme* (masculine) — the man
- *l'école* (feminine) — the school
- *l'ami(e)* — the friend

**les** never contracts to l' — it stays *les*: *les amis*, *les écoles*.

## Special cases

### Countries

Most countries are feminine (*la France*, *la Belgique*) or masculine (*le Canada*, *le Japon*). Countries used with *être* (to be) or *aller* (to go) often drop the article:

- *Je suis **en France**.* — I am in France. (not *dans la France*)
- *Elle va **au** Canada.* — She's going to Canada. (not *à le Canada*)
- *Les États-Unis **sont** grands.* — The United States are large.

### Abstract concepts and mass nouns

Even when general (not specific), French uses definite articles:

- *J'aime **le** chocolat.* — I like chocolate. (general preference)
- ***La** musique est importante.* — Music is important.

### Names of countries (continued)

After prepositions other than *à* and *de*, keep the article:

- *avec **la** France* — with France
- *pour **le** Canada* — for Canada
- *sans **les** États-Unis* — without the United States

## Common pitfalls

- Don't add *le/la* before abstract concepts when using verbs like *aimer* (to like) — *J'aime le chocolat* is correct, but *J'aime chocolate* in English already implies the general concept.
- Remember: *au* = *à + le*, *du* = *de + le*. Never write *à le* or *de le*.
