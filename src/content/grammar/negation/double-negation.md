## The principle: cumulative negatives

In French, **negatives stack and accumulate** rather than cancel each other out. This is fundamentally different from English.

**French**: *Je ne fais jamais rien.* = I never do anything. (two negatives = more negation)
**English**: "I don't never do nothing" = I do something. (two negatives = positive)

This means when you see multiple negative words in a French sentence, they all contribute to the negation — you add them together.

## Common combinations

### ne...jamais rien

Never + nothing — an emphatic "never anything":

*Je ne comprends jamais rien.* — I never understand anything.
*Tu ne fais jamais rien de constructif.* — You never do anything constructive.

### ne...personne...plus

Nobody + no longer — nobody still:

*Personne ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">vient</v> plus.* — Nobody comes anymore.
*Personne n'<v data-tense="present-indicatif">est</v> venu plus me voir.* — Nobody came to see me anymore.

### ne...ni...ni

Neither...nor — for two things that are both negated:

*Je n'ai ni faim ni soif.* — I'm neither hungry nor thirsty.
*Il ne parle ni ne écoute.* — He neither speaks nor listens.
*Elle n'<v data-tense="present-indicatif">achète</v> ni pain ni lait.* — She buys neither bread nor milk.

### ne...aucun(e)...plus

Not any + no longer:

*Je n'ai aucune raison de revenir plus.* — I have no reason to come back anymore.
*Elle n'a aucune excuse pour être en retard plus.* — She has no excuse for being late anymore.

## Position rules

In all these combinations:
- **ne** goes before the conjugated verb (or auxiliary in compound tenses)
- The negative particles after the verb follow their normal positions
- In compound tenses: *Je n'ai jamais rien fait.* — I never did anything.

| Structure | Position |
|-----------|----------|
| ne...jamais rien | ne + verb + jamais + rien |
| ne...personne...plus | ne + verb + personne + plus |
| ne...ni...ni | ne + verb + ni + element1 + ni + element2 |
| ne...aucun(e)...plus | ne + verb + aucun(e) + noun + plus |

## Why this matters: English vs French

In standard English, "I don't know nothing" is considered a double negative and means "I know something." Two negatives cancel.

French doesn't work this way:

| French | Literal | Actual meaning |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Je ne sais rien. | I don't know nothing | I know nothing |
| Je ne sais pas. | I don't know not | I don't know |
| Il ne fait jamais rien. | He doesn't never do nothing | He never does anything |

The moment you think "I'll cancel one negative," you misunderstand the sentence.

## More examples

*Je <v data-tense="present-indicatif">n'</v>ai jamais rien compris à la physique.* — I never understood physics.

*Personne ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">veut</v> rien entendre.* — Nobody wants to hear anything.

*Nous n'avons ni temps ni argent, et nous ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">pouvons</v> plus attendre.* — We have neither time nor money, and we can no longer wait.

*Elle ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">revient</v> jamais plus ici.* — She never comes back here anymore.

## When to use double negation

If one negative would work, adding a second is optional but common for emphasis. However, some combinations are required:

- **ne...ni...ni** is the only way to negate two items together
- **rien/jamais/personne + ne** at the start of a sentence requires the full structure
- **personne** as a subject always takes *ne* on the verb even when alone
