## What it is

The **ne...pas** structure is the standard way to make a sentence negative in French. The two particles bracket the conjugated verb. Other particles (plus, jamais, rien, personne, aucun, ni) work the same way.

## All core patterns

| Pattern | Usage | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|
| ne...pas | Basic denial | *Je ne sais pas.* — I don't know. |
| ne...plus | No longer | *Elle ne travaille plus ici.* — She no longer works here. |
| ne...jamais | Never | *Je ne mens jamais.* — I never lie. |
| ne...rien | Nothing | *Je ne vois rien.* — I see nothing. |
| ne...personne | Nobody | *Personne ne sait.* — Nobody knows. |
| ne...aucun(e) | Not any | *Je n'ai aucune idée.* — I have no idea. |
| ne...ni...ni | Neither...nor | *Je n'ai ni temps ni patience.* — I have neither time nor patience. |

## ne...ni...ni — Neither...nor

When negating two items together, use **ne...ni...ni**:

*Je n'ai ni faim ni soif.* — I'm neither hungry nor thirsty.

Both elements after the verb get **ni** before them:

*Il ne parle ni ne écoute.* — He neither speaks nor listens.

When the subject itself is negated with ni, **ne** is still required on the verb:

*Ni moi ni lui ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">comprend</v>.* — Neither me nor him understands.

### Placement rules

- **ne** goes before the conjugated verb
- **ni** goes before each negated element (which can be nouns, verbs, or adjectives)
- In compound tenses: *Je n'ai ni mangé ni dormi.* — I neither ate nor slept.

## Double negation — stacking negatives

French allows (and often requires) multiple negatives in the same sentence. Unlike English, where two negatives technically cancel, in French they **accumulate**:

*Je ne fais jamais rien.* — I never do anything. (literally: "I don't never do nothing")

### Common combinations

| Combination | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| ne...jamais rien | never + nothing | *Je ne comprends jamais rien.* — I never understand anything. |
| ne...personne...plus | nobody + no longer | *Personne ne <v data-tense="present-indicatif">vient</v> plus.* — Nobody comes anymore. |
| ne...aucun(e)...plus | not any + no longer | *Elle n'a aucune nouvelle plus.* — She has no news anymore. |

For a full explanation of combining negatives, see [Double Negation](/reference/grammar/negation/double-negation).

## Placement with infinitives

When the verb is in the infinitive form, the negative particles surround the infinitive:

*Ne pas faire* — not to do

*Je préfère <v data-tense="present-infinitif">ne pas partir</v>.* — I prefer not to leave.

In casual speech, **ne** is often dropped: *Pas faire ça.* — Don't do that.

## Position in compound tenses

In compound tenses (like passé composé), the negation wraps around the auxiliary verb:

*Je n'ai pas mangé.* — I didn't eat.
*Elle n'a jamais été là.* — She has never been there.
*Je n'ai ni vu ni entendu.* — I neither saw nor heard.

## Register: the dropping of ne

In **spoken French**, the **ne** is frequently omitted:

*Je sais pas.* — I don't know.
*Il vient plus.* — He doesn't come anymore.
*J'ai jamais vu ça.* — I've never seen that.

This is **not optional slang** — it's a real phonological phenomenon. The vowel in *ne* is weak and gets absorbed into the flow of speech. However, in **formal writing**, omitting *ne* is considered incorrect.

**Rule of thumb**: Write *ne* in anything that matters (essays, formal emails); drop it in casual conversation.

## Ne expletif — the "empty" ne

After verbs of **fearing**, **doubting**, and some expressions of fear, *ne* appears without negating anything:

*Je crains qu'il <strong>ne</strong> soit en retard.* — I fear he may be late.
*Il doute qu'elle <strong>ne</strong> dise la vérité.* — He doubts that she's telling the truth.
*J'ai peur qu'il <strong>n</strong>'arrive quelque chose.* — I'm afraid something might happen.

This *ne* adds no negative meaning — it's purely grammatical and cannot be dropped in formal French.

## Common pitfalls

- **Informal speech drops *ne***: *Je sais pas* is normal in conversation but wrong in writing.
- **_aucun(e)_** agrees in gender and number with the noun it negates: *aucune idée* (feminine).
- **rien** and **personne** can stand alone as subjects: *Personne ne sait* — Nobody knows.
- **Forgetting the second ni**: *Je n'ai ni faim ni soif* — NOT ~~*Je n'ai ni faim ni pas soif*~~
- **Trying to cancel negatives**: In French *ne...pas* + *rien* means "nothing" not "something". Two negatives intensify, they don't cancel.
