## What it is

The **ne...pas** structure is the standard way to make a sentence negative in French. The two particles bracket the conjugated verb. Other particles (plus, jamais, rien, personne, aucun) work the same way.

## All core patterns

| Pattern | Usage | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|
| ne...pas | Basic denial | *Je ne sais pas.* — I don't know. |
| ne...plus | No longer | *Elle ne travaille plus ici.* — She no longer works here. |
| ne...jamais | Never | *Je ne mens jamais.* — I never lie. |
| ne...rien | Nothing | *Je ne vois rien.* — I see nothing. |
| ne...personne | Nobody | *Personne ne sait.* — Nobody knows. |
| ne...aucun(e) | Not any | *Je n'ai aucune idée.* — I have no idea. |

## Placement with infinitives

When the verb is in the infinitive form, the negative particles surround the infinitive:

*Ne pas faire* — not to do

*Je préfère ne pas partir.* — I prefer not to leave.

In casual speech, **ne** is often dropped: *Pas faire ça.* — Don't do that.

## Position in compound tenses

In compound tenses (like passé composé), the negation wraps around the auxiliary verb:

*Je n'ai pas mangé.* — I didn't eat.
*Elle n'a jamais été là.* — She has never been there.

## Double negatives

You can combine negation particles:

*Je ne fais jamais rien.* — I never do anything. (both jamais and rien)
*Personne n'a dit rien.* — Nobody said anything. (Note: French allows multiple negatives)

## Common pitfalls

- **Informal speech** drops *ne*: *Je sais pas* is common in conversation but considered incorrect in formal writing.
- **_aucun(e)_** agrees in gender and number with the noun it negates: *aucune idée* (feminine).
- **rien** and **personne** can stand alone as subjects: *Personne ne sait* — Nobody knows.
