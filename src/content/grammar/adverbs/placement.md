## What it is

Where an adverb goes depends on what it modifies. Adverbs that modify verbs follow the verb. Adverbs that modify adjectives or other adverbs come before them.

## General rule: after the verb

The standard position for adverbs that modify verbs is right after the verb:

*Il <v data-tense="present-indicatif">travaille</v> <v data-form="adverb">dur</v>.* — He works hard.
*Elle <v data-tense="present-indicatif">voyage</v> <v data-form="adverb">souvent</v>.* — She travels often.

## Emphasis: beginning of clause

For emphasis or stylistic effect, many adverbs can move to the start of the sentence:

*Maintenant, je comprends.* — Now, I understand.
*Hier, nous sommes allés au cinema.* — Yesterday, we went to the cinema.
*Normalement, je ne fais pas ça.* — Normally, I don't do this.

## Multiple adverbs

When using more than one adverb, typical order is:

**manner** → **place** → **time**

*Il <v data-tense="present-indicatif">court</v> <v data-form="adverb">rapidement</v> <v data-form="adverb">dans le parc</v> <v data-form="adverb">le matin</v>.* — He runs quickly in the park in the morning.

## Adverbs modifying adjectives or other adverbs

These come before what they modify:

*Elle est <v data-form="adjective">extrêmement</v> <v data-form="adjective">intelligent</v>.* — She is extremely intelligent.
*Il parle <v data-form="adverb">très</v> <v data-form="adverb">vite</v>.* — He speaks very fast.

## Compound tenses

In compound tenses, the adverb usually goes between the auxiliary and the past participle:

*Elle a <v data-form="adverb">bien</v> <v data-form="adjective"> mangé</v>.* — She ate well.
*J'ai <v data-form="adverb">toujours</v> <v data-form="adjective">fait</v> ça.* — I've always done that.

## Common pitfalls

- Never place an adverb between the infinitive and its preposition: *penser à seriously* → *penser sérieusement*.
- Time adverbs like *hier, aujourd'hui, demain* can go at the start or end of a sentence without changing meaning.
