## What it is

**Syntax** covers how words combine to form sentences — particularly where different word types appear relative to each other. French word order is more rigid than English in some areas (pronoun placement, adjective position) but more flexible in others (subject position).

## Key differences from English

| Feature | French | English |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Adjective position | Often before noun (BAGS) | Almost always before noun |
| Pronoun placement | Before verb (COD/COS order) | After verb |
| Multiple pronouns | Strict sequence (MSSA-LL-YE) | Natural emphasis order |
| Negative sentences | Around verb: *ne...pas* | Before verb: *doesn't...* |
| Questions | Inversion or *est-ce que* | Question word order |

## The core principle

French prioritizes the **verb-object relationship** more strongly than English. Pronouns almost always precede the verb, and when multiple pronouns combine, they follow a fixed sequence that has no equivalent in English.

Understanding these rules will help you both **produce** correct French and **parse** what you hear or read.
