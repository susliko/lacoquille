The single hardest choice in French past tense — not because the rule is complex, but because it requires rethinking what "past" means.

## The core distinction

| | Imparfait | Passé composé |
|---|---|---|
| **View of action** | Ongoing, open-ended | Completed, bounded |
| **Role in narrative** | Background / setting | Foreground / event |
| **Frequency** | Habitual / repeated | One-off (or counted) |
| **State** | Describes a state | Records a change |

## The film metaphor

Think of a past narrative as a film:
- **Imparfait** = the establishing shot. The camera pans slowly. *It was raining. The street was empty. She was waiting.*
- **Passé composé** = the cut. A specific event happens and the story moves forward. *She saw him. She ran.*

Both tenses coexist in the same paragraph: the imparfait provides the canvas, the passé composé makes events happen on it.

## Decision tree

```
Is the action COMPLETED with a clear endpoint?
├─ Yes → Passé composé
└─ No → Is it a STATE (emotion, appearance, belief, weather)?
        ├─ Yes → Imparfait
        └─ Is it HABITUAL or REPEATED?
                ├─ Yes → Imparfait
                └─ Is it ONGOING / IN PROGRESS?
                        └─ Yes → Imparfait
```

## Signal words

**→ Imparfait:** *toujours, souvent, d'habitude, chaque jour, en général, autrefois, pendant des heures, quand j'étais jeune*

**→ Passé composé:** *soudain, tout à coup, un jour, finalement, ce matin-là, pendant dix minutes (= for a bounded duration), une fois, trois fois*

## Classic trap sentences

| Sentence | Which? | Why? |
|----------|--------|------|
| *Je mangeais quand il est arrivé.* | manger → imparfait; arriver → PC | Eating was ongoing (background); arrival is the event |
| *J'ai habité à Paris pendant deux ans.* | PC | Bounded duration with a clear endpoint |
| *J'habitais à Paris.* | Imparfait | Open-ended description of past state |
| *Elle a été malade pendant une semaine.* | PC | Bounded illness |
| *Elle était malade.* | Imparfait | Descriptive state, no endpoint given |
| *Hier, j'ai travaillé toute la journée.* | PC | "Hier" makes the day a completed, bounded event |

## The stative verb trap

Verbs like *savoir, croire, penser, vouloir, pouvoir, être, avoir, aimer* are inherently stative (they describe states, not actions). In the past, they almost always take imparfait:

*Je savais la réponse.* (not *j'ai su*… unless you mean "I suddenly found out")

But: *Quand j'ai su la vérité, j'ai pleuré.* — When I found out the truth (the moment of discovery), I cried. The PC signals the transition into the state.
