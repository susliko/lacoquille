## The Fundamental Question: Motion or State?

Before choosing among these seven verbs, first ask: **Is there motion happening?**

If the answer is **no** — someone is staying somewhere in a state of remaining — the choice is simple:

→ Use **rester**

*Je reste à la maison* — I'm staying home (a state, not motion)

---

## Motion Toward or Away from the Speaker?

If there **is** motion, the next question is: **Toward or away from the speaker (or reference point)?**

### *Aller* vs *Venir*

| Direction | French Verb | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Away from** speaker | **aller** | *Je vais à Paris* — I'm going to Paris (away from here) |
| **Toward** speaker | **venir** | *Je viens à toi* — I'm coming to you (toward you) |

The key insight: *aller* and *venir* are **perspective-dependent**. The speaker's position determines which verb to use.

*Je vais chez moi* (I'm going home) — because home is away from where I currently am.

*Je viens te voir* (I'm coming to see you) — because I'm approaching your location.

---

## Into or Out of an Enclosure?

If the motion involves crossing a **threshold** (entering or exiting a space):

### *Entrer* vs *Sortir*

| Direction | French Verb | Preposition | Example |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **Into** an enclosure | **entrer** | dans / en | *J'entre dans la maison* |
| **Out of** an enclosure | **sortir** | de | *Je sors de la maison* |

Think of a doorway: *entrer* is going **in through** it, *sortir* is going **out through** it.

---

## Departure or Arrival?

If the sentence emphasizes the **starting point** or **endpoint** of a journey:

### *Partir* vs *Arriver*

| Emphasis | French Verb | Preposition | Example |
|----------|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **Starting point** | **partir** | de | *Je pars de Paris* |
| **Endpoint** | **arriver** | à / chez | *J'arrive à Paris* |

*Partir* answers: "Where does this journey begin?"

*Arriver* answers: "Where does this journey end?"

---

## Common Confusion Points

### *Sortir* vs *Partir*

This is one of the most common errors among French learners:

- **Sortir** = to exit, to go out (through something) — *Je sors de la voiture*
- **Partir** = to leave, to depart (from somewhere) — *Je pars de la ville*

Both can be translated as "to leave" in English, but they emphasize different aspects:
- *Sortir* emphasizes the **physical exit** through/across a boundary
- *Partir* emphasizes the **departure event** from a location

### *Venir de* vs Simple Past

Learners sometimes confuse *venir de* + infinitive with the passé composé:

- *Je viens d'arriver* — I've just arrived (present tense + recent past meaning)
- *Je suis arrivé* — I arrived (simple past)

The construction *venir de* + infinitive uses the **present tense** but means something that happened in the recent past.

## Quick Decision Tree

```
Is there motion?
├─ No → RESTER (to stay/remains)
└─ Yes → Is the motion toward or away from speaker/reference?
         ├─ Toward → VENIR
         └─ Away → ALLER

Is the motion through a threshold (into/out of)?
         ├─ Into an enclosure → ENTRER
         └─ Out of an enclosure → SORTIR

Is the emphasis on departure or arrival?
         ├─ Departure point → PARTIR
         └─ Arrival point → ARRIVER
```

## The Seven Verbs at a Glance

| Verb | Meaning | Motion Direction | Key Preposition |
|------|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| **aller** | to go | away from speaker | à |
| **venir** | to come | toward speaker | de / à |
| **arriver** | to arrive | endpoint of motion | à |
| **partir** | to leave | origin of motion | de |
| **entrer** | to enter | into enclosure | dans |
| **sortir** | to exit | out of enclosure | de |
| **rester** | to stay/remain | no motion | à |
