## What it is

The **conditionnel présent** expresses what *would* happen under a condition, polite requests, or — crucially in journalism — unverified information the speaker doesn't vouch for. Despite its name, it's a mood, not a tense.

## When to use

- **Present/future hypothesis** (*si + imparfait* → conditionnel présent): *Si tu venais, ce serait super.* — If you came, it would be great.
- **Polite requests and suggestions**: *Pourriez-vous m'aider ?* — Could you help me? *Tu devrais appeler.* — You should call.
- **Wish or desire**: *J'aimerais partir en vacances.* — I'd like to go on holiday.
- **Unconfirmed / reported information** (journalistic conditional): *Le président aurait démissionné.* — The president is said to have resigned (unconfirmed).
- **Future from the past** (reported speech): *Il a dit qu'il viendrait.* — He said he would come.

## How to form

**Futur simple stem + imparfait endings**

| Person | Ending | *parler*    |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| je     | -ais   | parlerais   |
| tu     | -ais   | parlerais   |
| il     | -ait   | parlerait   |
| nous   | -ions  | parlerions  |
| vous   | -iez   | parleriez   |
| ils    | -aient | parleraient |

The stems are the same irregulars as the futur: *ser- (être), aur- (avoir), ir- (aller), fer- (faire)…*

## Common pitfalls

- **Si + conditionnel is wrong.** The condition clause always uses indicatif: *Si je pouvais* (not *si je pourrais*), *je le ferais*.
- **Confusing with imparfait**: both use the same endings. The difference is the stem — imparfait uses the nous-présent stem, conditionnel uses the infinitive/futur stem.
- **Journalistic conditional** is essential for reading French news. *Selon lui, il serait innocent* does not mean "he is innocent" — it means the source claims he is.
