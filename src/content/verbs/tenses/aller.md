## What it is

**Aller** means "to go" and is one of the most高频 high-frequency verbs in French. It is also entirely irregular — unlike most -er verbs which share a predictable pattern, aller must be memorised completely.

Beyond its literal meaning, aller serves as the auxiliary for the **futur proche** (near future): *Je vais partir* — I am going to leave / I will leave.

## How to form

### Present indicative (indicatif présent)

| Person | Form | Note |
|--------|------|------|
| je | **vais** | Irregular |
| tu | **vas** | Irregular |
| il/elle | **va** | Irregular |
| nous | **allons** | Regular -ons |
| vous | **allez** | Regular -ez |
| ils/elles | **va** | Same as il/elle |

The stem varies: **va-** for je/tu/il/nous/vous/ils — **all-** for nous/vous.

### Futur proche (near future)

**aller (present) + infinitive**

| Person | Futur proche |
|--------|--------------|
| je | vais partir |
| tu | vas manger |
| il/elle | va faire |
| nous | allons voir |
| vous | allez savoir |
| ils/elles | vont prendre |

*Je vais voir un film.* — I'm going to see a film. / I will see a film.

### Aller's own past: *aller* + infinitive is NOT used

To express "was going to do" in the past, French uses the **imparfait** of aller + infinitive — not a compound tense:

*Je savais qu'il **allait partir**.* — I knew he was going to leave.

## Common pitfalls

### Mixing up the two meanings

When you see *je vais*, check the context:
- *Je **vais** à Paris.* — I **am going** to Paris. (aller as main verb)
- *Je **vais partir**.* — I **am going to** leave. (aller as auxiliary for futur proche)

### In the past: imparfait + infinitive, not compound

English "was going to" uses past conditional. French uses **imparfait de aller** + infinitive:

*Il allait sortir quand j'ai appelé.* — He was about to go out when I called. (not *il était allé sortir*)
