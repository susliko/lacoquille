## What it is

**Rester** expresses a **state of remaining** in a place or condition — it describes where someone or something stays, not motion toward or away from somewhere. Unlike the other six verbs in this group, *rester* is fundamentally a **stative verb**, not a motion verb.

## Motion semantics

*Rester* answers the question "Where?" but implies **staying in place** rather than arriving or departing:

- *Je reste à la maison* — I'm staying at home
- *Elle est restée seule* — She remained alone

While the other motion verbs describe movement (*aller* — to go, *venir* — to come), *rester* describes **lack of movement**. It is the "non-motion" option in the movement verb group.

This is particularly important in past tense choices: *rester* in the past (especially imparfait) describes an ongoing state, while *partir* or *sortir* describe the event of leaving.

## *Être en train de* + infinitive

*Rester* works naturally with this periphrasis:

- *Je suis en train de rester* — I'm staying (emphasizing the ongoing nature)

However, because *rester* is already stative, this construction is less common than with action verbs.

## *Venir de* + infinitive

*Rester* does **not** combine with *venir de* in standard usage. The periphrastic construction *venir de* + infinitive expresses a recent action, which conflicts with *rester*'s stative nature of remaining in a place.

## Common expressions & idioms

- **Rester à** — to stay at, to remain at *Rester à la maison*
- **Rester chez soi** — to stay home (one's own place)
- **En rester là** — to leave it at that, to stop there *Restons-en là pour aujourd'hui*
- **Il reste que** — the fact remains that *Il reste que c'est difficile*
- **Rester sur** — to remain convinced of *Il est resté sur son idée*
