## What it is

**Partir** expresses **departure from a place** — the origin point of motion rather than the destination. While *arriver* asks "where does the motion end?", *partir* answers "where does the motion begin?" The starting point is the essential information.

## Motion semantics

*Partir* encodes **departure** from a reference point:

- *Je pars de Paris* — I'm leaving from Paris
- *Elle part à Londres* — She's leaving for London (the destination is London, the departure is "here")

The preposition **de** indicates the origin: *partir de* means "to leave from."

This contrasts with *arriver* (which emphasizes destination) and *sortir* (which emphasizes exiting through an enclosure).

## *Être en train de* + infinitive

*Partir* works naturally with this periphrasis:

- *Je suis en train de partir* — I'm in the process of leaving

This emphasizes the moment of departure, often used when you're saying goodbye.

## *Venir de* + infinitive

*Partir* combines naturally with *venir de*:

- *Je viens de partir* — I've just left
- *Elle vient de partir* — She just left

This expresses the recent completion of a departure.

## Common expressions & idioms

- **Partir de** — to leave from, to depart from *Le train part de Paris*
- **Partir pour** — to leave for (destination) *Je pars pour Lyon demain*
- **Partir en vacances** — to go on vacation *Nous partons en vacances samedi*
- **En partir** — to leave it, to have none left *Il n'en part plus rien*
- **À partir de** — starting from *À partir de demain, je suis libre*
