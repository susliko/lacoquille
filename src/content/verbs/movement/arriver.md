## What it is

**Arriver** expresses the **endpoint of motion** — the moment of arriving at a destination. Unlike *aller* which focuses on the journey itself, *arriver* focuses on the conclusion of that journey. The destination is the key element in the sentence.

## Motion semantics

*Arriver* answers the question "where to?" and emphasizes **reaching a destination**:

- *J'arrive à la maison* — I arrive at home
- *Le bus arrive* — The bus is arriving (coming into the stop)

This contrasts with *partir* (to leave from) which emphasizes the **origin** or **departure point**.

Think of *arriver* as answering: "Where does the motion end?" — The motion ends at the destination specified with *à* or *chez*.

## *Être en train de* + infinitive

*Arriver* works naturally with this periphrasis:

- *Je suis en train d'arriver* — I'm in the process of arriving

This emphasizes the final moments of approach, often when the destination is very close.

## *Venir de* + infinitive

*Arriver* combines naturally with *venir de* to express "to have just arrived":

- *Je viens d'arriver* — I've just arrived
- *Nous venons d'arriver à Paris* — We've just arrived in Paris

This is one of the most common and useful combinations.

## Common expressions & idioms

- **Arriver à** — to manage to, to succeed in *J'arrive à comprendre* — I manage to understand
- **Arriver à quelqu'un** — to happen to someone *Il m'est arrivé quelque chose*
- **Qu'est-ce qui arrive ?** — What's happening?
- **S'il arrive que** — if it happens that *S'il arrive que tu sois en retard…*
- **En arriver à** — to end up, to come to *J'en suis arrivé à croire que…*
