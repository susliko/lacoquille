## What it is

**Venir** expresses motion **toward** the speaker's location or toward a specified reference point. While English "to come" and "to go" both translate to French, the choice between *venir* and *aller* is not arbitrary — it depends on the speaker's position relative to the destination.

## Motion semantics

*Venir* encodes motion **toward** a reference point. This reference point can be:

- The speaker's current location: *Viens ici !* — Come here!
- Another person's location: *Je viens te voir* — I'm coming to see you
- A place significant to the listener: *Je viens de Paris* — I'm from Paris (literally: "I come from Paris")

The key contrast is with *aller*, which describes motion **away from** the speaker.

## *Être en train de* + infinitive

*Venir* works naturally with this periphrasis:

- *Je suis en train de venir* — I'm on my way coming

This emphasizes the ongoing nature of the approach.

## *Venir de* + infinitive

This is one of the most useful periphrastic constructions in French:

- *Je viens d'arriver* — I've just arrived
- *Elle vient de partir* — She just left

This construction expresses the **recent past** — an action completed just moments ago. It uses the present tense of *venir* + de + infinitive, not a past tense.

## Common expressions & idioms

- **Venir de** — to come from (origin) *Je viens de France*
- **Venir de** + infinitive — to have just (done something) *Je viens de manger*
- **Venir chercher** — to come pick up/get *Je viens te chercher*
- **Venir voir** — to come see *Venez nous voir demain !*
- **En venir à** — to get around to, to come to *Qu'est-ce que tu viens faire ici ?*
