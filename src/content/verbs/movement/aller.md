## What it is

**Aller** is one of the most essential French verbs — it expresses motion away from the speaker's location toward some destination. Unlike English "to go," French *aller* always implies movement away from where the speaker (or reference point) currently is.

## Motion semantics

*Aller* encodes **directional motion away from the speaker**. The destination is the key information:

- *Je vais à l'école* — I'm going to school (away from here)
- *Elle va chez elle* — She's going home (away from here toward her home)

This contrasts with *venir*, which describes motion **toward** the speaker or reference point.

## *Être en train de* + infinitive

*Aller* works naturally with this periphrasis to express an action in progress:

- *Je suis en train d'y aller* — I'm on my way there.

This construction emphasizes the ongoing nature of the journey rather than its completion.

## *Venir de* + infinitive

*Aller* does **not** combine naturally with *venir de* + infinitive, since *venir de* already expresses coming/arrival. You cannot say *\*je viens d'aller* to mean "I just went" — that construction doesn't exist in French.

To express a recent completed journey, use the passé composé: *Je viens de rentrer* (I've just come back) rather than trying to use *aller* with *venir de*.

## Common expressions & idioms

- **Aller bien/mal** — to be doing well/badly *Comment allez-vous ? — Je vais bien.*
- **Ça va ?** — How's it going? (informal greeting)
- **Aller chercher** — to go get/pick up *Va chercher tes frère !*
- **S'en aller** — to leave, to go away (reflexive emphasis)
- **Y aller** — to go (there), often in instructions *Allez-y !* — Go ahead!
